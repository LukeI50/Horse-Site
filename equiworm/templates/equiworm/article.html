Worming Smarter: What Every Horse Owner Needs to Know About Anthelmintic Resistance

What is Anthelmintic Resistance?

Anthelmintic resistance is when parasites (worms) develop the ability to survive treatment with deworming medications (known as anthelmintics). In other words, the wormer that once worked to kill parasites becomes ineffective. Just like bacteria can become resistant to antibiotics, worms can adapt and become resistant to dewormers. This means that over time, the tools we rely on to protect our horses’ health could stop working.

Why Is Anthelmintic Resistance a Growing Problem?

For many years, the traditional approach to worm control was regular, blanket worming: treating all horses at routine intervals regardless of need. While this was well-intentioned, we now know it is a major cause of anthelmintic resistance. Here’s why:

  1. Stronger worms survive:

    Each time a dewormer is used, it kills the most susceptible worms. However, some resistant worms may survive and pass on their resistance to the next generation.

  2. Overusing wormers speeds this up:

    Frequent, unnecessary treatments apply constant pressure, accelerating the development of resistance.

  3. No new wormers are on the horizon:

    There hasn’t been a new class of equine wormers developed in decades, so we must protect the effectiveness of the ones we have.

The result? Worm populations are increasingly harder to control, putting your horse at risk of parasitic disease. Studies, such as those by Relf et al. (2014) and Kaplan & Nielsen (2010), have shown significant resistance in small strongyles (cyathostomins), the most common equine intestinal parasites.

Why Should You Care?

Anthelmintic resistance doesn’t just affect individual horses, it has wider consequences:

  1. Health Risks for Your Horse:

    Horses with high worm burdens are at risk of colic, weight loss, and other severe diseases. Resistant worms make it harder to treat these problems effectively.

  2. Increased Costs:

    If wormers stop working, owners may face higher costs for testing, treatments, and managing worm-related diseases.

  3. Environmental Impact:

    Wormers, especially macrocyclic lactones like ivermectin and moxidectin, can harm dung beetle populations and other beneficial insects (Beynon et al., 2015). These insects play a vital role in breaking down manure and maintaining healthy pastures.

By acting now, you can protect your horse and the environment while preserving the effectiveness of wormers for the future.

How Can You Be Part of the Solution?

The good news is that anthelmintic resistance can be slowed down with smarter, evidence-based worming practices. Here are some simple steps every horse owner can take:

  1. Test Before You Treat

    • Use Fecal Egg Counts (FEC) to check if your horse needs worming. Many horses don't need treatment all the time.

    • Test results will show whether your horse is shedding high levels of worm eggs and needs a targeted treatment.

    • Reference: Nielsen et al., 2021 showed that regular testing reduces unnecessary wormer use and resistance.

  2. Follow a Risk-based Approach

    • Horses have different worm risks based on age, health, and environment.

    • For example:

      • Young horses (under 5) are more susceptible to parasites.

      • Older horses with weak immune systems need closer monitoring.

      • Healthy adult horses often shed fewer eggs and may not need frequent treatments.

    • The CANTER Guidelines recommend tailoring treatment based on your horse’s individual needs rather than using a “one size fits all” approach.

  3. Use the Right Wormer at the Right Time

    • If treatment is needed, choose the correct wormer for the specific parasite problem.

    • Work with your vet to ensure you’re using an appropriate product. Avoid rotating wormers unnecessarily, as this can also contribute to resistance.

  4. Manage Your Pasture

    • Reduce worm burdens on pasture with good management practices, such as:

      • Poo-picking regularly:

        Remove droppings at least twice a week.

      • Rotating grazing:

        Move horses to fresh paddocks to break worm life cycles.

      • Co-grazing with sheep or cattle:

        This reduces worm burdens by disrupting the parasites’ preferred host.

    • Reference: Studies by Corbett et al. (2014) highlight the effectiveness of faecal removal for reducing pasture contamination.

  5. Monitor Wormer Effectiveness

    • Use a Faecal Egg Count Reduction Test (FECRT) to check if wormers are working. If egg counts remain high after treatment, it could indicate resistance.

    • Testing ensures you’re not wasting time or money on ineffective products.

  6. Record and Plan Your Worming Strategy

    • Keep records of worm tests and treatments to monitor your horse’s parasite control.

    • Use a tool like our Equine Worm Risk Assessment to simplify this process and guide responsible worming decisions.

The Bottom Line

Anthelmintic resistance is a real and growing problem, but horse owners like you can make a difference. By switching to a targeted, risk-based approach and adopting good pasture management, you can protect your horse, save money, and safeguard wormers for future generations.

Treat smarter, not more often.
